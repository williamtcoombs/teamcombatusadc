Bottom Closed Guard Position (-1.0)

The bottom closed guard position refers to when a practitioner is on their back with their legs wrapped and locked around their opponent’s waist, controlling them from the bottom. This position allows the bottom player to limit the top person’s movement and posture while setting up attacks such as sweeps, submissions (e.g., armbars, triangles, chokes), and transitions. It is a powerful control position where leverage, angles, and grips are used to break the opponent’s posture and create offensive opportunities. Success from closed guard depends on active engagement, grip control, and the ability to manage distance and timing while threatening attacks or advancing to a more dominant position.

Bottom Closed Guard Principles

1. Breaking Posture

Constantly work to break your opponent’s posture by pulling them forward or controlling their head, collar, or sleeves. A broken posture limits their ability to pass or strike and opens the door for attacks.

2. Grip Control

Establish and maintain strong grips on the sleeves, collar, or wrists to control your opponent’s movement and set up submissions or sweeps.

3. Angle Creation

Use hip movement, underhooks, and leg positioning to shift your body off-center. Angling creates opportunities to attack and makes it harder for the top player to defend or pass.

4. Active Engagement

Don’t stay static—use your legs, hips, and arms to control, off-balance, and constantly threaten. A passive guard allows the opponent to posture and break free.

5. Threats and Attacks

Chain together submissions (like armbars, triangles, and omoplatas) and sweeps to keep the top player defensive and reactive. The more you threaten, the harder it is for them to safely open your guard.

6. Hip Mobility and Core Engagement

Good closed guard relies on being able to elevate, pivot, and shift your hips quickly. Strong core engagement helps with both defense and transitions.

7. Guard Retention and Recovery

If your opponent starts to open your guard, use frames, knee shields, or re-gripping to transition to open guard variations and maintain control.

Bottom Open Guard Position (-1.01)

The bottom open guard position refers to when the practitioner is on their back or seated with their legs not closed around the opponent, but rather open and actively controlling distance, posture, and angles. Unlike closed guard, the legs are used independently to hook, frame, and engage in a variety of guards—such as spider, De La Riva, lasso, or butterfly. Success from open guard relies on dynamic hip movement, strong grip control, and the ability to constantly threaten sweeps or submissions. This position offers high versatility but requires proactive defense and adjustment to maintain control.

Bottom Open Guard Principles

1. Distance Management

Use your legs, hips, and grips to manage space and prevent guard passes. Control the range to stay safe while launching attacks.

2. Active Framing and Posting

Employ your arms and legs to frame against your opponent and create structural resistance that helps maintain your guard.

3. Angle Creation

Constantly adjust your hips and shoulders to form attacking angles that improve leverage for sweeps and submissions.

4. Grip Control

Secure and maintain grips on sleeves, pants, or collar to limit your opponent's movement and set up offensive opportunities.

5. Mobility and Hips Engagement

Stay active with your hips—lift, shift, and turn as needed to adapt and counter your opponent’s pressure or passes.

6. Connection and Hooks

Use your feet and legs to hook, press, or engage with your opponent’s limbs to maintain control and manipulate their base.

7. Continuous Threat

Keep your opponent on the defensive with the constant possibility of sweeps or submissions. Threats prevent them from settling or passing easily.

Bottom Half Guard Position (-2)

The bottom half guard is a versatile and strategic position in Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu where the practitioner controls one of the opponent’s legs while maintaining strong frames and hip positioning from underneath. It is often used to neutralize pressure, slow down passing attempts, and launch sweeps or back takes. The effectiveness of this position relies on staying on your side, controlling distance with frames, and fighting for dominant grips—particularly the underhook. With proper structure, timing, and movement, bottom half guard serves as a transitional hub for offense and defense.

Bottom Half Guard Principles

1. Win the Underhook

The underhook on the far side gives you control, allows you to come up to your knees, and prevents the top player from flattening you or applying crossface pressure.

2. Maintain Proper Head Position

Keep your head tight under your opponent’s jaw or on their ribs. Avoid letting your head get pushed to the mat—this kills mobility and sets up passes.

3. Frame with Elbows and Arms

Use frames across the hips, biceps, or neck to create space and protect your alignment. Keep your inside elbow in tight to avoid giving up chest-to-chest pressure.

4. Knee Shield and Leg Engagement

Engage your outside leg as a knee shield when needed, and keep your inside leg entangled around their leg. Active legs block passes and initiate offense.

5. Angle and Hip Movement

Don’t stay flat—rotate onto your side and use your hips to create angles. This opens paths for sweeps and avoids getting crushed by top pressure.

6. Connection and Control

Stay connected with grips and hooks. A tight connection gives you control of their posture and prevents explosive movements or quick passes.

7. Threaten Sweeps

Keep your opponent reacting by attacking with sweeps like old school, knee tap, or deep half entries. Offense slows their passing attempts.

8. Transition Wisely

Use the position as a launchpad—transition to dogfight, full guard, deep half, or back takes. Don’t remain static or defensive.

Bottom Turtle Position (-3)

The bottom turtle position in Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu is typically a transitional and defensive posture where the practitioner is on all fours with elbows and knees on the mat. While it may seem vulnerable, a disciplined turtle can be an effective position for defending against back takes and submissions, as well as creating opportunities to recover guard, stand up, or reverse the position. Mastery of the turtle involves minimizing openings, anticipating your opponent’s moves, and moving decisively when space appears.

Bottom Turtle Principles

1. Protect the Neck

Keep your chin tucked and your hands close to your neck at all times. Your first priority is defending against chokes—don't let them sneak in the collar or seatbelt.

2. Elbow and Knee Connection

Stay compact with elbows and knees close together. This structure helps block hooks, prevent arm isolation, and maintain defensive posture.

3. Stay Compact and Mobile

Avoid flattening out or becoming static. Keep your weight centered and be ready to move dynamically to defend or escape.

4. Hide the Handles

Tuck in your arms and avoid giving up easy grips on collars, wrists, or belt. Don’t let your opponent latch onto easy control points.

5. Block Hooks and Seatbelt

Proactively defend against hook insertion and seatbelt grips. Peel off grips early, rotate as needed, and stay ahead of back take attempts.

6. Use Movement Wisely

Escape with intent—don’t panic or explode blindly. Sit outs, rolls, and guard recoveries should be timed with your opponent’s movement or momentary imbalance.

7. Wrestle Back to Base

When possible, work to stand up or wrestle to a more neutral or dominant position. Don’t settle—turtle is a temporary staging ground.

8. Awareness of Weight Shifts

Feel your opponent’s pressure. Use their movements—like reaching for grips or shifting weight—to time your escapes or reversals.

Bottom North-South Position (-4)

The bottom north-south position occurs when your opponent is chest-to-chest on top, facing your head. It is a highly controlling pin if left unmanaged. Your job on bottom is to prevent submissions, manage pressure, and escape back to a guard or turtle position. Effective survival from bottom north-south relies on good structure, timing, and composure. Awareness of your opponent’s weight shifts and grip intentions is critical for creating escape opportunities.

Bottom North-South Principles

1. Protect the Neck and Arms

Keep your chin tucked and your arms tight to avoid north-south chokes or kimura setups. Don’t reach out or extend unnecessarily.

2. Frame and Create Structure

Build frames with your hands, forearms, and elbows against your opponent’s hips, shoulders, or head to prevent full control and open space for escape.

3. Avoid Being Flat

Get off your back by turning onto your side or shoulder. Being flat gives your opponent full pressure and makes framing difficult.

4. Head Positioning

Keep your head turned toward your opponent if possible, and protect it from isolation. Watch for their arms reaching for grips or chokes.

5. Escape the Hips, Not Just the Head

Moving your hips is key to recovering guard or creating space. Head movement alone won’t free you—use your core and frames to shift your body.

6. Timing and Sensitivity

Wait for weight shifts or grips to be committed. Then move decisively—sit up, hip escape, or roll. Escape when they’re adjusting.

7. Prioritize Guard Recovery

Your main goal is to recover to full guard, half guard, or turtle. Always look for space and angle to bring your knees back inside.

8. Breathing and Composure

Stay calm under pressure. Breathe through the squeeze, conserve energy, and look for openings to re-frame and escape. Panic leads to bad choices.

Bottom Side Control Position (-5)

The bottom side control position in Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu is a common but vulnerable scenario where your opponent has passed your guard and is controlling your upper body and hips. Your main priorities are survival, regaining structure, and recovering guard. Good posture, timely framing, and smart escapes can neutralize your opponent’s pressure and open opportunities to recover or reverse.

Bottom Side Control Principles

1. Frame Immediately

Use your arms, forearms, and hands to create frames against their neck, shoulders, or hips. Frames reduce pressure and give you breathing room to move.

2. Stay on Your Side

Avoid lying flat—rotate slightly onto your side. This posture gives you better movement and makes it harder for them to pin you fully.

3. Protect Inside Space

Keep your elbows and arms close. Don’t let them get underhooks, isolate your arms, or walk your limbs overhead—this leads to submissions.

4. Head and Neck Safety

Block the crossface early. Once they control your head, your spine alignment is compromised and escapes become harder.

5. Hip Movement (Shrimping)

Effective escapes start with your hips. Use bridging and shrimping to move your hips away and slide your bottom knee back in to recover guard.

6. Guard Recovery Priority

Your primary goal is to bring your legs back between you and your opponent. Work toward full guard, half guard, or even turtle to reset your defense.

7. Use Underhook or Elbow Escape

Fight for an underhook to turn in or use the elbow escape to slide your frame in and recover space. Both options help you transition safely.

8. Stay Calm Under Pressure

Manage your breathing and avoid wasting energy. Stay composed so you can time your escape when the opportunity arises.

9. Anticipate Transitions

Be alert for shifts to mount, knee-on-belly, or north-south. Adjust your frames and hips early to block or counter the next attack.

Bottom Knee-on-Belly Position (-6)

The bottom knee-on-belly position is a high-pressure, transitional control point where your opponent drives their knee into your torso while keeping their other foot posted for balance and mobility. From the bottom, your goals are to survive the pressure, defend against submissions, and create movement to recover guard or escape. While uncomfortable, proper structure and timing can neutralize the threat and turn the tables.

Bottom Knee-on-Belly Principles

1. Frame Immediately

Create a strong frame using your arms against their knee, hip, or torso. Frames relieve pressure and give you room to move.

2. Control Their Knee

Redirect their knee off your torso with hand pressure or hip rotation. Prevent them from sliding across your chest and increasing pressure.

3. Angle Your Body

Turn slightly onto your side to reduce pressure on your core and protect your breathing. Angles create opportunity for movement.

4. Keep Elbows and Arms Tight

Don’t let your arms flare—this opens you up to armbars, kimuras, or collar chokes. Keep your arms tight to your body.

5. Bridge and Shrimp

Combine a strong bridge with a hip escape to create space and knock your opponent off balance. Use it to recover guard or sit up.

6. Avoid Pushing Straight Up

Don’t push directly against their chest—it wastes energy and exposes submissions. Focus on controlling the knee and using leverage.

7. Recover Guard or Turtle

Your best exits are recovering a leg-based guard or turning to turtle. Keep moving and don’t accept the position passively.

8. Time Your Escape

Wait for a shift in weight or a grip change—then move decisively. Poor timing leads to failed escapes and further control.

9. Stay Calm Under Pressure

Don’t panic. Control your breathing and focus on structure and timing. Composure is key to escaping knee-on-belly.

Bottom Technical Mount Position (-7)

The bottom technical mount position occurs when your opponent shifts to a seated mount, often behind one of your arms, with one knee behind your back and the other in front. It’s a highly threatening pin that leads to submissions or transitions to full mount or back control. To defend effectively, you must manage frames, protect your neck and arms, and find ways to disrupt their control and recover guard or turtle.

Bottom Technical Mount Principles

1. Protect the Neck and Near Arm

Keep your chin down and arms tight to prevent chokes and armlocks. This is a prime attack position for the top player—protect your upper body first.

2. Stay on Your Side

Avoid being flattened. Turn onto your side, facing away from your opponent, to keep your core structure engaged and mobile for escapes.

3. Use Top Elbow and Knee as Frame Points

Frame across your body with your elbow and connect it to your top-side knee. This frame blocks hip pressure and creates a wedge for defense.

4. Bridge to Disrupt Their Balance

Well-timed bridging (upa) can destabilize your opponent and create space for you to move or insert a leg to recover guard.

5. Clear the Hooked Leg

The opponent often has their leg threaded behind your top-side leg. Trapping or clearing that leg is crucial to escaping the position.

6. Reconnect Knees and Elbows

Bring your knees and elbows back toward each other to close space and defend. This posture helps prevent back takes and allows guard recovery.

7. Be Ready to Turn In or Away

Depending on their pressure and positioning, you may need to turn into them to recover guard or turn away to reach turtle safely.

8. Time Your Escape With Their Movement

Wait for their weight to shift or as they go for a grip—then move decisively. Poor timing results in tighter control or submission traps.

9. Stay Calm and Composed

This is a high-pressure position. Control your breathing and stay mentally focused. Escapes are possible if you maintain structure and patience.

Bottom Mount Position (-8)

The bottom mount is one of the most vulnerable positions in Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu, where your opponent is seated on your torso with full control and attacking potential. Your primary objective is to stay calm, defend against submissions, and escape to a better position. Success comes from structured defense, effective timing, and clean technical movement—not brute force.

Bottom Mount Principles

1. Protect the Neck and Arms

Keep your elbows close to your ribs and your hands near your neck. Prevent easy access to collar chokes and armlocks by staying tight and compact.

2. Avoid Being Flat

Rotate slightly to one side to create angles for escape. Being flat gives your opponent full pressure and limits your movement options.

3. Trap and Bridge (Upa Escape)

Trap your opponent’s arm and foot on one side, then explode with a bridge to roll them over. Timing and control are key to avoid being countered.

4. Elbow-Knee Escape

Shrimp your hips away and slide your knee inside to recover half guard or full guard. This escape works well when they’re tight and balanced.

5. Frame Effectively

Use your hands, elbows, or forearms to frame against their hips or chest. Frames create breathing room and help you set up escapes.

6. Stop the High Mount

Keep your elbows low and block their knees from sliding up your torso. High mount leads to submissions and severely limits escape options.

7. Bait Carefully (Advanced)

At higher levels, you can sometimes bait your opponent into overcommitting. Use this with caution and only if you can defend and counter effectively.

8. Time Escapes with Their Movement

Wait for them to adjust posture, attack, or shift weight—then move. Static escapes rarely work; use their movement against them.

9. Stay Calm and Conserve Energy

Mount pressure can feel overwhelming. Breathe, relax your muscles, and defend intelligently. Panicking wastes energy and creates openings for attacks.

Bottom Back Mount Position (-9)

The bottom back mount is one of the most dangerous positions in Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu. Your opponent has full control from behind and direct access to your neck and arms. The priority is to survive the initial attack, strip grips, and escape using precise timing and structured movement. A calm, layered defense is key to surviving and eventually reversing or escaping the position.

Bottom Back Mount Principles

1. Hand Fighting First

Defending your neck is the top priority. Constantly monitor and strip grip attempts before they can be secured. No escape matters if you're getting choked.

2. Two-on-One Control

Control their top arm (the choking arm) with both of your hands. This control gives you time and space to work out of the position safely.

3. Tuck Your Chin, Lower Your Head

Protect your neck by tucking your chin and lowering your head beneath their shoulder line. It limits their choke angles and buys time to escape.

4. Keep Your Back Off Their Chest

Slide your back down and away to disconnect from their chest. This weakens their control and begins the process of breaking alignment.

5. Clear the Hooks

Use your hips, feet, and hands to remove one of their hooks. Clearing a hook reduces their stability and opens a route to escape.

6. Turn Toward the Overhook Side

Rotate toward the arm over your shoulder (the overhook). This is usually the safer direction to turn and leads to better escape pathways.

7. Use the Mat

Try to bring your back to the mat during escapes. Once flat, your opponent’s back control weakens and your escape options increase.

8. Stay Calm and Breathe

Don’t panic, even under tight pressure. Control your breathing, stay composed, and trust your layered defense to give you time to escape.

9. Recognize Traps and Baits

Don’t reach wildly or expose your arms. Experienced opponents will bait reactions to set up submissions. Stay disciplined in your defense.
