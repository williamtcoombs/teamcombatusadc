Provided below is a hierarchy for classical BJJ positions, where the assigned score reflects positional advantage. Higher numbers indicate greater control. For example, Top Back Mount (+9) is preferable to Standing (0) which is preferable to Bottom Back Mount (-9). We provide the leg entanglement position hierachy when we get to that content.

Principles of Top Control

  • Base before pace: keep three points connected before moving.
  • Head–hips alignment: stacked spine = hard to tip.
  • Near-side pin, far-side track: kill near shoulder/hip; monitor far elbow/knee.
  • Hands are hazards: manage and see both hands.

Top Back Mount Position (+9)

The top back mount position is one of the most advantageous positions, offering both control and submission potential. From this position, the practitioner is behind the opponent with chest-to-back contact, typically using a seatbelt grip and hooks or a body triangle to control the hips. The focus is on maintaining tight connection, staying behind the shoulders, and neutralizing escape attempts by adjusting grip, pressure, and angle. With the opponent’s mobility limited, submissions like the rear naked choke or bow and arrow choke become highly effective. Success in this position relies on balance, constant adjustment, and strategic hand fighting.

Top Back Mount Position Principles

1. Seatbelt Control (Harness Control)
- One arm over the shoulder, one under the armpit, hands locked together.
- Hide the choking arm behind the head.
- Keep the seatbelt tight and chest glued to their back.
2. Hooks or Leg Control
- Use both legs (hooks) inside the thighs to control hips.
- Alternatives: body triangle or hip clamp.
- Main goal: immobilize their hips.
3. Head & Shoulder Alignment
- Keep your head close to theirs, ideally on choking side.
- Stay behind their shoulders—like a backpack.
4. Control Before Submission
- Don’t rush the choke—secure the position first.
- Keep hooks tight, chest-to-back connection strong.
5. Chin Is Not a Defense
- Don’t stop attacking because of the opponent’s chin.
- Chokes can be applied around or through the chin with correct technique.
6. Connection & Pressure
- Maintain tight chest-to-back contact.
- Apply consistent pressure using upper body and leg control.
7. Anticipate Escapes
- Watch for escape attempts—like turning to the mat.
- Follow their movements and adjust your hooks and seatbelt.
8. Angle Management (Diagonal Control)
- Control from a slight angle to improve choking leverage.
- Avoid being flat behind them—makes attacks easier, defense harder.
9. Choke Readiness
- Be ready to attack: RNC, bow and arrow, collar chokes, etc.
- Flow from control to submission without losing position.



Top Mount Position (+8)

The top mount position is a dominant control position where you sit on your opponent’s torso with your knees on the ground, allowing you to apply pressure, isolate limbs, and attack submissions while remaining relatively safe. Effective top mount control involves keeping your hips low, knees tight, and chest heavy to limit your opponent’s movement. You must control their hips and head, use your hands for balance and control, and constantly threaten transitions or submissions to maintain pressure. A high mount under the armpits or S-mount variation can further reduce their defense and open opportunities to finish the fight.

Top Mount Position Principles

1. Control the Hips

Block the opponent’s hips with tight knees, grapevines, or low mount pressure. Their hips are the source of their escapes.

2. Control the Head and Upper Body

Pin their shoulders with chest pressure, underhooks, or crossface. Keep their upper body flat on the mat.

3. Keep Your Knees Tight

Flared knees create space. Keep them tight to maintain contact and prevent shrimping escapes.

4. Balance and Base Are Crucial

Be ready to post or shift your weight to counter bridging and rolling. Base before pressure.

5. Climb to a High Mount

Advance to a high mount under their armpits. It reduces their leverage and opens submissions.

6. Use Your Hips to Apply Pressure

Let your hips sink. Sit heavy and make them carry your weight to tire them and reduce movement.

7. Isolate Limbs for Submission

Once controlled, isolate arms or shoulders using grapevines, crossfaces, and transitions to submissions.

8. Stay One Step Ahead of Escapes

Read their intentions. Counter early before the escape builds momentum.

9. Threaten Submissions to Maintain Control

Threats force reactions. Use chokes and armlocks to create openings and pin their focus.

10. Transition Smoothly If Necessary

If you lose control, flow into side control, knee-on-belly, or back mount. Prioritize dominant position over ego.

Top Technical Mount Position (+7)

The top technical mount position is a refined variation of the standard mount, designed to maintain control while minimizing the risk of being reversed. In this position, one knee stays tight behind the opponent’s upper back or shoulder, while the other foot is posted out for base and balance. This setup allows for strong chest-to-back pressure, excellent control over the near-side arm, and seamless transitions to submissions or back takes. The technical mount is especially effective for isolating limbs, applying pressure, and adapting to an opponent's defensive movements without sacrificing position.

BJJ Technical Mount Principles

1. Weight Distribution and Base

Stay heavy on their chest and shoulders. Use your posted foot for balance, and keep your hips low and tight to maintain control and prevent sweeps.

2. Control the Near Arm and Shoulder

Use a seatbelt grip or underhook to isolate the near arm, setting up submissions and reducing their ability to defend.

3. Head and Upper Body Control

Keep your chest glued to their shoulder or back, with your head low to eliminate space and maintain dominant positioning.

4. Knee and Leg Placement

Your top knee should wedge behind their upper back; your posted leg provides base and prevents them from bridging or rolling you.

5. Transition Threats

From technical mount, threaten back takes, armbars, triangles, or transitions to S-mount to keep them reacting.

6. Preventing Escapes

Monitor their far elbow and hips—stay tight to prevent shrimping or turning. Base out with your foot to stop bridging.

7. Positional Awareness

Be ready to switch sides, reset mount, or transition to the back depending on their reaction. Stay fluid and intentional.

Top Knee-on-Belly Position (+6)

The top knee-on-belly position in Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu is a dynamic control point where one knee presses into the opponent’s torso while the other leg is posted for balance and mobility. This position allows for intense pressure, movement, and quick transitions while keeping the opponent under stress. Key principles include driving weight through the knee, maintaining chest-to-chest pressure, and using grips like underhooks or crossfaces to limit their frames. It’s commonly used to force reactions, set up submissions, or transition to mount or back control, making it a powerful tool for both control and attack.

Knee-on-Belly Principles

1. Proper Knee Placement

Your knee should rest near their sternum or solar plexus. Avoid placing it on their hips or belly button to maximize control and pressure.

2. Foot Position (Posted Leg)

The non-knee leg is posted out for balance and movement. Keep your foot wide and angled outward to prevent being swept.

3. Weight Distribution

Apply pressure through your knee into their chest, while staying light on your posted foot. This makes it hard for them to breathe or move.

4. Active Core & Hip Engagement

Stay mobile and responsive by engaging your core and hips. Float on top of them rather than settling into a static posture.

5. Head and Arm Control

Use collar grips, crossface, or underhooks to immobilize their upper body and prevent them from pushing your knee off or turning into you.

6. Transitions & Attacks

Be ready to transition to mount, side control, or back control. Threaten submissions like armbars, baseball bat choke, or paper cutter choke.

7. Preventing Escapes

Anticipate frames and shrimping. If they bridge hard or turn into you, float with your foot and switch sides or step off to maintain control.

8. Use Pressure to Force Reactions

Knee-on-belly isn’t passive—apply pressure to create discomfort. This often opens submission opportunities as they react.

Top Side Control Position (+5)

Top Side Control Position Principles

1. Control the Hips

Block their hip movement using your body positioning, knees, or pressure to prevent escapes like shrimping or re-guarding.

2. Kill the Near-Side Arm

Control or trap their inside arm to eliminate framing. Use shoulder pressure, crossface, or underhook to neutralize it.

3. Chest-to-Chest Pressure

Maintain tight chest contact to keep them flat. Use shoulder pressure to turn their head away and limit mobility.

4. Head and Arm Position

Use an underhook on the far arm and crossface the head. This isolates their upper body and improves control.

5. Stay Low and Wide

Keep your hips low and knees wide. This base helps prevent being bridged, rolled, or reversed.

6. Use the Crossface

A solid crossface breaks their posture and makes it hard for them to bridge or turn in. Keep consistent shoulder pressure.

7. Stay Mobile, Not Static

Adjust based on their frames and reactions. Be ready to switch sides, go to north-south, mount, or knee-on-belly.

8. Monitor Their Energy

Make them carry your weight. Stay patient and wear them down—wait for openings or mistakes.

9. Threaten Submissions

Keep them defensive with submission threats like Kimura, Americana, arm triangle, or lapel chokes.



Top North-South Position (+4)

The top north-south position is a strong control position where the practitioner’s body is aligned head-to-head with the opponent, often chest-to-chest, with hips low and arms controlling the opponent’s shoulders or arms. This position limits the bottom person's mobility and visibility, making it difficult for them to frame or escape. Key elements include applying downward pressure, maintaining tight connection, and controlling the arms to prevent guard recovery. North-south is also a launchpad for submissions like the north-south choke, kimura, or transitions back to side control or mount. It emphasizes control, pressure, and patience.

North-South Control Principles

1. Hip and Chest Pressure

Keep your hips low and drive your chest into their upper torso to control movement and limit their breathing.

2. Head-to-Hip Alignment

Position your head closer to their hips than their chest. This improves control and prevents bridging escapes.

3. Control the Arms

Use underhooks or wrist control to prevent frames and set up submissions like kimuras or north-south chokes.

4. Keep Your Elbows In

Stay compact with elbows tight to prevent escapes and maintain a strong structure.

5. Stay Mobile

Be ready to transition to side control, mount, or knee-on-belly as needed. Stay dynamic and responsive.

6. Use Submission Threats

Threaten submissions like the north-south choke or kimura to force reactions and create openings.

7. Head Control or Escape Block

Use your body to block their attempts to turn, frame, or recover guard. Keep them flat and neutralized.

8. Kill the Hips

Drive your weight into their hips and core to eliminate their ability to generate escape power.

Top Turtle Position (+3)

The top turtle position is a transitional yet powerful attacking position. It involves controlling an opponent who is on all fours, often while looking to advance to the back or secure submissions. Mastering this position means maintaining pressure, preventing escapes, and threatening multiple attacks while staying balanced and connected.

Top Turtle Principles

1. Control Before Submission

Don’t rush attacks. Establish grips like seatbelt, harness, or double underhooks before attempting submissions. Prioritize position over finishing.

2. Weight Distribution

Keep your weight heavy and centered on their hips and shoulders. Avoid leaning too far in any direction to prevent sweeps or rolls.

3. Threaten Multiple Attacks

Keep them guessing with back takes, chokes, arm traps, or leg entries. Don’t let them settle or rest in the position.

4. Block Their Escapes

Use your limbs to seal off sit-outs, rolls, or guard recoveries. Maintain tight control around the hips and elbows.

5. Hooks and Leverage

Insert inside or outside hooks to control the legs or hips. Use your legs and hips—not brute strength—for breakdowns and transitions.

6. Balance and Base

Keep a wide, mobile base to prevent being rolled. Stay aware of their movement and post as needed with your hands, knees, or elbows.

7. Grip Fighting

Hand fighting is key. Break their grips, peel hands, and control wrists to open submission paths or prevent their escapes.

8. Stay Tight and Sticky

Don’t give them space. Stay glued to their back or side with constant pressure and movement, like a weighted backpack.

Top Half Guard Position (+2)

Top half guard is a key transitional position, offering control and pressure while working to pass your opponent’s remaining leg entanglement. Effective use of the top half guard requires positional awareness, head and arm control, pressure, and dynamic passing options. Mastery of this position can neutralize strong guard players and lead to dominant outcomes.

Top Half Guard Principles (BJJ)

1. Win the Underhook

Getting the underhook on the far side is crucial. It allows forward pressure, limits their mobility, and sets up passes or transitions to mount or the back.

2. Control the Head Position

Keep your head lower than theirs, near their jaw or temple. This maintains top pressure and prevents them from building posture or sweeping.

3. Use Crossface and Shoulder Pressure

Apply your shoulder across their face to make them turn away. This breaks alignment and posture, setting up an easy path to pass.

4. Flatten Their Shoulders

Keep them flat on the mat to eliminate leverage. A turtled or side-lying opponent is harder to pin or pass.

5. Knee and Leg Positioning

Your free knee must be active—either slicing, stapling, or windshield wiping through. Don’t leave it idle or let it get trapped.

6. Prevent Deep Half or Lockdown

Keep your hips heavy and your trapped leg free from being pulled into deep half guard or lockdown. Clear hooks before advancing.

7. Remove Frames

Use your arms and pressure to collapse their inside frames. Without frames, their ability to create space or push you off is limited.

8. Control Their Hips

Use chest-to-hip pressure and crossface mechanics to keep their hips pinned. If their hips are mobile, they can escape or recover guard.

9. Stay Active, Not Passive

Don’t stall. Constantly apply pressure, adjust angles, and threaten passes. A passive top half guard gives them time to recover.

10. Chain Passing Options

Be ready to switch passing strategies—knee slice, smash pass, leg weave, or back step—based on their reactions. Adapt and flow.

Top Open Guard Position (+1.01)

The top open guard position refers to when a practitioner is on top of an opponent who is using an open guard—meaning their legs are not locked around the torso (as in closed guard) but are instead actively controlling distance and positioning through hooks, grips, and frames. From the top, the goal is to maintain balance and posture while neutralizing the bottom player’s grips and mobility, often by controlling the legs, hips, and sleeves or pant legs. The top player works to pass the guard by breaking through or around the legs, aiming to achieve dominant positions such as side control or mount, all while defending against sweeps and submissions from the bottom.

Top Open Guard Principles

1. Posture and Balance

Maintain a strong base and upright posture to resist sweeps and submissions. Stay centered with weight distributed to avoid overcommitting.

2. Grip Fighting

Control or break the opponent’s grips to limit their ability to off-balance, sweep, or submit. Secure your own grips to initiate passing sequences.

3. Distance Management

Stay just far enough to avoid being entangled in submissions or sweeps, or close enough to pressure and control the hips and legs—constantly adjusting based on the guard type.

4. Leg and Hip Control

Neutralize the opponent’s legs through pinning, stapling, or redirecting them, as their legs are their primary tools for retention and attack.

5. Angle and Pressure

Work to create angles that expose the guard player’s side or back and apply consistent pressure to wear them down and force reactions.

6. Mobility and Timing

Stay light and mobile, ready to sprawl, switch directions, or retreat if needed. Timing is key to exploiting transitions or guard recovery attempts.

7. Passing Focus

Always work toward passing the guard methodically rather than simply stalling; keep chaining pass attempts and stay proactive.

Top Closed Guard Position (+1.0)

The top closed guard position refers to when a practitioner is inside their opponent’s closed guard—where the bottom player has their legs wrapped and locked around the top player’s waist. From this position, the top player is in a vulnerable but neutral situation, as the bottom player can launch sweeps, submissions, and positional attacks. The top player’s primary objectives are to maintain strong posture, control the opponent’s hips and grips, and work to open the guard safely without exposing themselves to attacks. Once the guard is opened, the top player can begin passing to more dominant positions such as side control or mount. Maintaining balance, avoiding forward pressure, and using methodical movement are key to neutralizing threats and progressing offensively.

Top Closed Guard Principles

1. Posture Control

Maintain a strong, upright posture with your head above your hips and back straight to reduce vulnerability to submissions like triangles, armbars, and collar chokes.

2. Grip Fighting

Constantly break the opponent’s grips and establish your own to prevent them from setting up attacks and control the tempo of the exchange.

3. Hip Control

Keep the opponent’s hips pinned or controlled—either with your arms, elbows, or body position—to limit their ability to angle off or elevate for sweeps and submissions.

4. Base and Balance

Stay centered with a solid base by keeping your knees wide and feet active. Avoid leaning forward or back excessively, which can compromise stability.

5. Guard Opening Strategy

Focus on safe, structured methods to open the guard, such as standing up or using pressure-based techniques, while maintaining posture and avoiding submission traps.

6. Pressure and Patience

Apply consistent pressure to force reactions and fatigue the opponent. Don’t rush—open the guard methodically while staying calm and defensive.

7. Threat Awareness

Stay constantly aware of submission threats, especially chokes, armbars, and sweeps. Be ready to disengage or adjust your position at the first sign of danger.

Transitions & Chains

  • Side → KOB → Mount → Technical Mount → Back Mount
  • Mount ↔ Technical Mount ↔ Back exposure
  • KOB → North‑South → Side (opposite)

Training Drills

  1. 30‑second ride (no grips change) in Side Control.
  2. Mount ladder (low → high) with bumps.
  3. Flow chain rounds through the ladder.

Safety & Context

Minimum force necessary. Communicate, control hands, and disengage when possible. Respect gym rules and local law.
