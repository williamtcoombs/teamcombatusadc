History

"The past does not repeat itself, but it rhymes." — Mark Twain

Combatives, a term broadly referring to military hand-to-hand combat systems, has roots in ancient warfare where soldiers trained in close-quarters fighting techniques for battlefield survival. These systems evolved over centuries, drawing from traditional martial arts and battlefield tactics.

In the modern era, particularly during World War II, combatives were formalized by military instructors like William E. Fairbairn and Rex Applegate, who developed practical, efficient techniques for Allied forces. These methods emphasized disabling an opponent quickly and effectively, often with minimal rules.

Over time, military combatives programs, such as the U.S. Army's Modern Army Combatives Program (MACP), incorporated elements from boxing, wrestling, jiu-jitsu, and other martial arts to adapt to both battlefield and peacekeeping scenarios.

Today, combatives remain a core component of military training, focusing on real-world applicability, situational awareness, and combat readiness.
