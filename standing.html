Standing Position (0)

The stand‑up is our “Position 0.” Build a stance that lets you move, deny grips, and threaten entries without overcommitting. Think: athletic base, posture tall, hands purposeful.

Standing Position Principles

1. Strong Stance & Balance

Feet shoulder‑width, lead foot slightly forward, knees soft. Hips under shoulders; weight on the balls of your feet. Test: partner gives a gentle tug—if you don’t have to step, you’re balanced.

2. Grip Fighting

Win hands before you win takedowns. Inside ties beat outside ties. Clear sleeves/wrists immediately; re‑grip with intent.

  • Rule of twos: break with two hands, establish with two hands.
3. Posture Control

Head tall, chin tucked, spine neutral. Your forehead or jawline wins head position; your elbows stay in to protect frames.

4. Footwork & Angles

Small steps beat lunges. Step‑replace to recover stance; pivot to create outside angles; circle away from their power side.

5. Takedown Threats

Carry two A‑options you trust (e.g., outside trip + single leg) and one B‑option (e.g., knee‑tap). Chain them.

6. Level Changes

Hips drop by bending the knees, back stays flat, eyes up. Hands follow hips, not the other way around.

7. Distance & Timing

Be in or out—avoid the reaching middle. Enter when they blink (post‑break, grip change, or stance reset).

8. Safe Guard Pulling

If you elect to pull guard, do it with posture and grips established; sit or jump only onto soft mats with a cooperative partner.

Entries & Setups

  • Footwork first: step‑replace to recover stance; pendulum steps to cover distance; pivot to create the outside angle.
  • Hand fighting: clear grips immediately, re‑grip with inside control (biceps/wrist). Snap‑down → front headlock or angle off.
  • Level change: back flat, eyes up, knee touches softly; drive through, don’t reach.
  • Feints: sell the shot to open trips/throws; sell the throw to open the shot. Touch, don’t chase.

Chains to try: collar‑tie snap → outside trip • wrist drag → single leg • post‑break → knee‑tap.

Clinch & Control Ties

  • Collar tie / inside tie: break posture with your forearm; elbow inside to win pummeling; step to the corner, not straight back.
  • Underhook / overhook: underhook side drives the chest; opposite head position controls posture; step behind to off‑balance.
  • 2‑on‑1 (Russian tie): pull to your hip, circle to their back or finish with a trip when they square up.
  • Body lock: gable grip at the elbows, hips in, step outside and sag to finish without slamming.

Principle: head position + hip line beat arm strength. Move their posture before moving their feet.

Takedown Families

  • Leg attacks: single‑leg (high‑c, run the pipe), double‑leg (penetration step, angle off, lift & turn).
  • Trips & reaps: outside/inside trip, sasae‑style foot blocks, foot sweeps off grip breaks and pulls.
  • Hip throws: enter deep, hip line under center; load to your belt line, rotate with controlled descent.
  • Clinched finishes: knee‑tap, ankle pick, body‑lock outside step—prioritize balance over amplitude.

Choose a base game: one trip, one leg attack, one clinch finish. Train the chain, not the move.

Breakfalls & Recovery

  • Back breakfall: chin tucked, slap the mat, engage hips to rock up to a seated guard.
  • Side breakfall: elbow tight, shoulder/lat absorb; chamber legs to recover guard or stand.
  • Technical stand‑up: frame, post hand, kick‑through; eyes on partner; stand into stance.

Practice on soft mats first; quality of the fall determines how fast you can re‑engage or disengage.

Training Drills

  1. Grip fight rounds (3×60s): establish & clear grips only, no takedowns. Focus on inside control and head position.
  2. Entry chains (3×90s): feint → level change → finish with light resistance. Swap roles each rep.
  3. Wall rounds (3×60s): pummel on the barrier; finish with knee‑tap or outside trip. Reset on heavy contact.
  4. Breakfall circuit (3 rounds): 5 back + 5 side falls → technical stand‑ups → stance reset. Smooth before fast.

Keep rounds light and technical; save intensity for positional sparring with clear constraints.

Safety & Context

  • Use the minimum force necessary; avoid high‑amplitude throws on hard or crowded surfaces.
  • Communicate before increasing pace; partners release immediately on verbal or physical tap.
  • Prefer controls that keep partners’ heads/neck/knees safe—finish by balance and angle, not by spike.
