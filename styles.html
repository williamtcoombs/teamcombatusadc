Styles

“Discipline is choosing between what you want now and what you want most.” — Abraham Lincoln

There are many factors to consider when choosing a fighting style. These include physical factors such as body type and proportions, reach, flexibility, strength, endurance, speed, age, and history of injuries or limitations.
There are cognitive factors such as temperament, stress management, preferred learning style, tolerance for complexity, adaptability, and whether you thrive on structure or improvisation. Evaluating these dimension helps align training with your natural strengths while identifying areas worth cross-training for balance. Additionally, consider the fighting styles taxonomy categores provided below to insure your your training plan is consistent with your short- and long-term objectives

Fighting Styles Taxonomy

  • 1) Armament
  • 2) Primary Method
  • 3) Range / Phase
  • 4) Context / Rules
  • 5) Equipment & Environment
  • 6) Tactical Scope
  • 7) Training Modality
  • 8) Mechanics Flavor

1) Armament

  • Unarmed
  • Contact weapons : impact (stick/club), edged (knife/sword), flexible (rope/chain/whip), rigid (staff/polearm), shield, dual‑wield, thrown
  • Projectile : archery/throwing; modern firearms

2) Primary Method (what wins)

  • Striking (punches, kicks, knees, elbows)
  • Clinch (tie‑ups, dirty boxing, knees/elbows inside)
  • Throws/Takedowns (off‑balancing, pickups, sweeps)
  • Grappling control (pins, rides, positional dominance)
  • Submissions (joint locks, chokes)
  • Weapons mechanics (cuts, thrusts, binds, deflections)

3) Range / Phase

Projectile → Kicking → Punching → Clinch → Ground (top) → Ground (guard/entanglement)

4) Context / Rules

  • Combat sport
  • Self‑protection (civilian)
  • Battlefield/Military
  • Law‑enforcement/Security
  • Duel (weapon or unarmed)
  • Ritual/Performance/Health

5) Equipment & Environment

  • Armor : none / light / heavy
  • Mobility : on‑foot / mounted / aquatic / confined‑space
  • Protective gear : none / light / competition‑legal

6) Tactical Scope

  • 1v1 vs multiple attackers
  • Integrated (mixes phases/weapons) vs specialized (single phase)

7) Training Modality (“aliveness”)

  • Forms/Kata, structured drills
  • Cooperative flow
  • Sparring / Randori
  • Pressure testing / Competition
  • Scenario work / Force‑on‑force (weapons)

8) Mechanics Flavor (optional)

  • Hard/external vs soft/internal
  • Footwork‑centric / Ground‑centric / Weapon‑centric

Examples (alphabetical)

Boxing
Armament: Unarmed; Method: Striking; Range: Punching; Context: Sport; Gear: light; Scope: 1v1; Training: sparring/competition.
Brazilian Jiu‑Jitsu
Unarmed; Control+Submissions; Ground; Sport & self‑protection; gi/no‑gi; 1v1; rolling/competition.
Capoeira
Unarmed; Striking+Evasion; Kicking/footwork; Ritual/performance & self‑protection; multiple‑attacker awareness; roda + sparring variants.
HEMA (longsword, etc.)
Contact weapons; Weapons mechanics+Ringen (grappling); Duel/Battlefield; variable armor; integrated; sparring/competition.
Japanese Jiu‑Jitsu
Unarmed (with weapon adjuncts historically); Throws+Joint locks+Strikes; Clinch→Ground; Battlefield origins & self‑protection; traditional gi; integrated; kata, randori, and scenario training.
Judo
Unarmed; Throws+Grappling control; Clinch→Takedown; Sport; gi; 1v1; randori/competition.
Kali/Escrima/Arnis
Contact weapons + Unarmed; Weapons mechanics+Striking+Grappling; Close; Self‑protection/Duel; integrated; drills/flow/sparring.
Kendo
Contact weapons (sword, armored); Weapons mechanics; Duel; Sport; heavy armor; 1v1; competition.
Modern CQB
Contact & Projectile weapons; Weapons mechanics+Grappling+Striking; Close/room‑clearing; Military/LE; armor/firearms; team scenarios; force‑on‑force.
Muay Thai
Unarmed; Striking+Clinch; Kicking/Punching/Clinch; Sport & self‑protection; 1v1; high aliveness.
Sambo
Unarmed; Throws+Grappling (+Striking in Combat Sambo); Clinch→Ground; Sport/Military; 1v1; competition.
