Styles

‚ÄúDiscipline is choosing between what you want now and what you want most.‚Äù ‚Äî Abraham Lincoln

There are many factors to consider when choosing a fighting style. These include physical factors such as body type and proportions, reach, flexibility, strength, endurance, speed, age, and history of injuries or limitations.

There are cognitive factors such as temperament, stress management, preferred learning style, tolerance for complexity, adaptability, and whether you thrive on structure or improvisation. Evaluating these dimensions helps align training with your natural strengths while identifying areas worth cross-training for balance.

Additionally, consider the categories provided below to ensure your training plan is consistent with your short- and long-term objectives.

Fighting Styles Taxonomy

1) Armament

  • Unarmed
  • Contact weapons: impact (stick/club), edged (knife/sword), flexible (rope/chain/whip), rigid (staff/polearm), shield, dual-wield, thrown
  • Projectile: archery/throwing; modern firearms

2) Primary Method (what wins)

  • Striking (punches, kicks, knees, elbows)
  • Clinch (tie-ups, dirty boxing, knees/elbows inside)
  • Throws/Takedowns (off-balancing, pickups, sweeps)
  • Grappling control (pins, rides, positional dominance)
  • Submissions (joint locks, chokes)
  • Weapons mechanics (cuts, thrusts, binds, deflections)

3) Range / Phase

Projectile ‚Üí Kicking ‚Üí Punching ‚Üí Clinch ‚Üí Ground (top) ‚Üí Ground (guard/entanglement)

4) Context / Rules

  • Combat sport
  • Self-protection (civilian)
  • Battlefield/Military
  • Law-enforcement/Security
  • Duel (weapon or unarmed)
  • Ritual/Performance/Health

5) Equipment & Environment

  • Armor: none / light / heavy
  • Mobility: on-foot / mounted / aquatic / confined-space
  • Protective gear: none / light / competition-legal

6) Tactical Scope

  • 1v1 vs multiple attackers
  • Integrated (mixes phases/weapons) vs specialized (single phase)

7) Training Modality (‚Äúaliveness‚Äù)

  • Forms/Kata, structured drills
  • Cooperative flow
  • Sparring / Randori
  • Pressure testing / Competition
  • Scenario work / Force-on-force (weapons)

8) Mechanics Flavor (optional)

Each style expresses its mechanics through one or more of the following flavors:

  • Hard / External ‚Äì power from muscular force and direct impact (e.g. Muay Thai, Boxing).
  • Soft / Internal ‚Äì power from timing, relaxation, and redirection (e.g. Tai Chi, Aikido).
  • Footwork-centric ‚Äì mobility and angles define offense and defense (e.g. Savate, Capoeira).
  • Ground-centric ‚Äì control and submissions from positional grappling (e.g. BJJ, Wrestling).
  • Weapon-centric ‚Äì range and leverage shaped by tool use (e.g. Kali, Kenjutsu).

This flavor label helps describe how a system moves or generates power, complementing the examples below.
