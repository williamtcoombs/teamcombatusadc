Insights Worth Knowing

An overview, the importance of inside control, the 32 principles, white and blue belt priorities, and positional hierarchy.

Overview

Danaher partitions fundamentals into 3 areas, each with 5 corresponding fundamental skills. This approach is easy to understand, implement, and utilize as a framework for instruction. It includes standing [stance, motion, grip, kuzushi (off balancing), transitioning to the ground], ground position bottom (pin escapes, guard retention, guard sweeps, half guard sweeps, turtle escapes), and ground position top (opening closed guard, passing open guard, passing half guard, pin maintenance & transitions, turtle breakdowns).

The Importance of Inside Control

Learn how vital it is to control the area below the chin, above the knees, and beneath the elbows of your opponent.

The 32 Principles

The 32 principles of Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu offer a conceptual framework to guide both instruction and performance. They go beyond technique to shape how students think about movement, timing, control, and adaptation. Principles like Connection, Distance, and Centerline help students understand spatial dominance, while Balance, Anchor, and Mobility support structural integrity. Others, such as Overload, Depletion, and Redirection, illuminate strategies for breaking down resistance. Concepts like Sacrifice, False Surrender, and Pivot encourage creativity and tactical awareness. When taught deliberately, these principles accelerate comprehension, deepen retention, and foster a style of grappling that is responsive, intelligent, and transferable across positions and rule sets. For instructors, they are invaluable tools for organizing curriculum, diagnosing problems, and cultivating principled fighters rather than rote technicians.

White Belt and Blue Belt Priorities

Beginners should prioritize building rock-solid foundations in positional escapes, control, and high-percentage submissions rather than chasing flashy moves or explosive power. Mastering escapes from pins and holding dominant positions consistently sets the stage for long-term growth. Efficiency, technical control, and defensive awareness are far more important at early stages than aggressiveness or resorting to strength-based tactics.

Classical BJJ Positional Hierarchy

Positions are conjugate pairs where, with the exception of the symmetric class (e.g., standing, kneeling, 50/50 guard), one position is superior in terms of offensive and defensive potential. We have partitioned positions into four categories (i.e., top positions, bottom positions, standing, leg entanglement positions) to aid conceptualization. As a general strategy, seek to improve your position, looking for submission opportunities as they arise.

We provide a relative position metric, where a positive score denotes the superior position; whereas, a negative score denotes the inferior. For example, top mount position has been assigned a score of "+8" because it is the dominant/preferred position and bottom mount position has a score of "-8" because it is inferior. A position score is best thought of as a range of scores, where realignment might change the advantage/disadvantage proportion in say the "50/50 guard" to 60/40 or 70/30 depending on who has slightly improved control of the knee and access to a dangling ankle. Position ratings represent ordinal-scale approximations of relative desirability. The utility of a transition may be computed by subtracting the starting from the ending position, so, moving from bottom mount position to bottom half guard position is a 6-point [i.e., (-2)-(-8) = 6] improvement in position. This is conceptual guidance, not the scoring system used in tournaments. It would be misleading to interpret these metrics too literally, as the metrics differ for each practitioner. If, for example, you are a Darce choke aficionado, then those positions where there is easier access (e.g., side control position, turtle position) could be bumped up a little, whereas if you are weak in a position, let's arbitrarily choose bottom guard position, then your personal guard score should be reduced. This metric is an obvious simplification, as are labels for positions, as there are many variations within any given position. In each case, they are attempts to aid understanding.

To muddy the waters further, the scores differ depending on the rules of engagement. For example, John Danaher considers the closed guard position to be neutral (0.0), which is a rational argument in the context of a Brazilian jiu-jitsu tournament, where striking is forbidden, whereas in a mixed martial arts competition where striking is permissible, the striking advantage tilts the scales in favor of the top closed guard individual. This complexity of quantification increases swiftly as additional fight factors (e.g., weapons, terrain, multiple assailants) are considered.

Safety & Hygiene

Safety Rules

  • Tap fast, release faster. No neck cranks or heel hooks for beginners.
  • Protect partners’ joints; no spikes or uncontrolled slams.
  • Tell your partner/coach about injuries or limits before rounds.

Hygiene

  • Clean gi/rashguard every session. No exceptions.
  • Trim nails; cover cuts; shower ASAP after training.
  • Leave the room better than you found it.
