Leg Entanglement Positions

Positional Hierarchy

This section outlines the modern Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu leg entanglement hierarchy, based on control, transition potential, and submission reliability. Positions are listed from most to least dominant.

1. Inside Sankaku / Saddle / 411 / Honey Hole
The pinnacle of leg entanglement positions. Provides elite closed-circuit inside control, shuts down counterattacks, and offers the most effective access to inside heel hooks.
2. Inside Ashi Garami (Reap / Leg Knot)
A strong inside entanglement achieved via leg reaping. Offers tight control and transitions smoothly into Saddle. Difficult to escape and highly effective for submissions.
3. Outside Ashi Garami (Double Outside Ashi)
A powerful closed-circuit outside entanglement. Offers good control and access to submissions like the outside heel hook, though more susceptible to counters than inside control positions.
4. 80/20 Guard
An upgraded version of the 50/50 guard that provides asymmetric control. The dominant player retains most of the control while minimizing risk of counterattacks.
5. 50/50 Guard
A symmetrical entanglement where both practitioners have mirrored control. Considered neutral unless leveraged strategically into more dominant positions.
6. Cross Ashi Garami (Topside / Diagonal Ashi)
A transitional position that offers moderate control. Used to shift between entanglements, but less secure than closed-circuit variations.
7. Ashi Garami (Standard / Single-Leg X)
The most basic and least dominant position. Commonly used as an entry point for leg entanglements, but offers minimal control unless upgraded.

Inside Sankaku Position

The inside sankaku is the most dominant leg entanglement position in modern Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu. It is favored for its control, safety from counters, and submission versatility.

Inside Sankaku Principles

1. Inside Leg Control (Closed Circuit)

Your entangling leg threads inside their legs, forming a closed triangle or figure-four. This provides superior control and limits your opponent’s counter-attacks.

2. Heel Hook Entry and Breaking Mechanics

The Saddle gives you clean access to inside heel hooks—the most powerful submission from this position. It also allows for controlled application of torque.

3. Hip and Knee Isolation

The opponent’s leg is trapped at the hip and knee. This isolation prevents their ability to spin, slip, or build frames effectively.

4. Safety from Counter Leg Attacks

Unlike 50/50 or Outside Ashi, the Saddle protects your own legs by keeping your entangling leg safe from being attacked.

5. Versatile Transitions

You can seamlessly transition from the Saddle to back takes, knee bars, toe holds, or mount. It’s a launching pad for advanced control and attacks.

6. Submission Reliability

This position gives you some of the highest finishing rates in competition due to the ease of isolating and applying heel hooks.


Inside Ashi Garami Position

The inside ashi garami, often referred to as the leg knot or achieved via the reap, is a powerful closed-circuit leg entanglement in which the attacker’s entangling leg crosses inside their opponent’s legs, with the opponent’s knee turned inward (reaped) across their centerline. This positioning isolates the opponent’s leg while offering excellent rotational control, making it difficult for them to escape or counter. Unlike the more neutral outside or open entanglements, Inside Ashi provides direct access to high-percentage submissions like the inside heel hook while minimizing the risk of being countered. It also serves as a transitional gateway into the saddle (inside sankaku), and is a favored configuration in modern no-gi systems due to its balance of control, safety, and submission efficiency.

Inside Ashi Garami Principles

1. Inside Leg Positioning
The entangling leg threads between the opponent’s legs and crosses the hip line from the inside, making it harder for them to escape or pummel out. This positioning grants superior control over their lower body.
2. Closed-Circuit Control
A locked configuration (typically triangle or figure-four) restricts movement and stabilizes the entanglement. Closed-circuit control prevents leg slips and enables stable submissions.
3. Knee Line Control
Maintaining the opponent’s knee inside the entanglement is crucial. If the knee escapes past the hips, the position and threat of submission are lost.
4. Reap for Breaking Mechanics
The reaping action turns the opponent’s knee inward across their centerline, compromising posture and enhancing the breaking mechanics of inside heel hooks.
5. Heel Exposure
Proper control and rotational positioning force the opponent’s heel to face upward or outward, allowing quick access to devastating heel hooks.
6. Connection and Weight Distribution
Staying connected to the opponent’s hips and maintaining inward pressure prevents disengagement. Leaning toward the far hip helps secure the entanglement and maintain balance.
7. Transitional Potential
Inside Ashi Garami is an ideal launchpad for advancing into the Saddle, finishing leg locks, or even taking the back when the opponent attempts to roll or defend.

Outside Ashi Garami Position

The outside ashi garami is a closed-circuit leg entanglement where the attacker’s legs wrap around the opponent’s leg from the outside, with the entangling leg crossing over the opponent’s hip and the feet typically triangled or figure-foured to secure control. This position provides strong immobilization of the opponent’s leg and excellent access to submissions like the outside heel hook, straight ankle lock, and knee bar. While it offers good breaking mechanics and rotational pressure, it’s less structurally secure than inside entanglements due to greater exposure to counters and back takes if not tightly controlled. Outside Ashi is especially useful in no-gi, submission-only grappling, and is often used as a finishing position or transitional step toward stronger configurations like the Saddle.

Outside Ashi Garami Principles

Outside Ashi Garami is a powerful closed-circuit leg entanglement used to control and submit opponents from the outside of their leg. Below are the foundational principles that define this position's structure and effectiveness:

1. Outside Leg Positioning
The attacker’s entangling leg wraps around the outside of the opponent’s leg, typically crossing over the hip. This creates an initial angle for control and submission pressure.
2. Closed-Circuit Configuration
The legs are locked together (often in a triangle or figure-four), creating a closed circuit around the opponent’s leg. This enhances positional stability and reduces their ability to slip out.
3. Foot and Heel Isolation
This position excels at isolating the opponent’s foot and heel, enabling submissions like the outside heel hook and straight ankle lock. Proper rotational control is crucial for effective finishes.
4. Knee Line Retention
Maintaining the opponent’s knee line within your entanglement is essential. If their knee escapes, the position is compromised and submission threats vanish.
5. Angle of Control
Establishing an effective angle limits the opponent’s ability to square up or pressure forward. This control angle also maximizes torque during submissions and protects against counters.
6. Positional Vulnerabilities
Because the control comes from the outside, this position is more vulnerable to counters like back takes or knee slides if control lapses. Strong grips and tight frames are critical for safety.
7. Transition Readiness
Outside Ashi is commonly used as a transitional platform to reach more dominant entanglements like Saddle or 50/50, especially when the opponent reacts defensively or attempts to roll out.

80/20 Guard Position

The 80/20 guard is a positional variation of the 50/50 guard in Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu where one athlete gains a significant control advantage—approximately 80%—while the opponent retains only limited, defensive options—roughly 20%. Unlike the symmetrical nature of 50/50, the 80/20 guard involves angling the hips, controlling grips, and manipulating leg positioning to dominate the entanglement. This setup allows the controlling player to isolate the opponent’s leg for submissions (such as heel hooks or straight ankle locks) while keeping their own leg safely hidden and out of reach. It’s a modern, strategic evolution of the leg entanglement game, designed to neutralize risk while maintaining offensive momentum.

80/20 Guard Principles

The 80/20 guard is a modern evolution of the 50/50 position, designed to tilt the balance of control heavily in favor of one player. These principles explain how to dominate the position safely and effectively.

1. Asymmetry of Control
The 80/20 guard breaks the symmetry of the 50/50 by shifting control toward one practitioner. By creating angles and limiting the opponent’s mobility, you gain dominant leverage while they remain mostly defensive.
2. Heel Hiding and Exposure
A core principle is keeping your own heel safely tucked while exposing the opponent’s heel. This allows you to attack confidently with heel hooks or ankle locks while minimizing your own risk.
3. Knee Line Management
Trapping the opponent’s knee line inside your entanglement is essential. Once their knee escapes, they can slip out of the position and negate your submission opportunities.
4. Angle Adjustment
Constant angle control is key. Shifting your hips or elevating slightly changes the dynamic, keeping you in control and preventing the opponent from reestablishing symmetry or applying pressure.
5. Grip and Frame Control
Effective use of grips and frames—such as posting on the hip or controlling the opponent’s far leg—prevents movement, limits counters, and reinforces your control advantage in the position.
6. Attack Flow
The 80/20 guard is an attacking position, not a holding one. Use the imbalance in control to launch submissions, sweeps, or transitions before the opponent can recover or reset the symmetry.

50/50 Guard Position

The 50/50 guard is a symmetrical leg entanglement position where both practitioners have their legs interlocked around each other’s, creating mirrored offensive and defensive opportunities. Each player has equal access to attacks like heel hooks, ankle locks, and sweeps, making the position both versatile and dangerous. Its neutral nature means that neither competitor has a clear control advantage unless one shifts the angle or posture—such as transitioning into an 80/20 variant. While valuable in both gi and no-gi grappling, the 50/50 guard is often criticized for enabling stalling, especially in points-based competition, and requires precise strategy to convert into a dominant or submission-oriented position.

50/50 Guard Principles

The 50/50 guard is a mirrored leg entanglement where both players share equal offensive and defensive potential. These core principles highlight the strategic demands and vulnerabilities of this neutral position.

1. Symmetrical Entanglement
Both players have identical leg configurations wrapped around each other’s legs, giving each equal opportunities for offense and defense. The position is balanced by design.
2. Neutral Control
50/50 offers no inherent control advantage to either player. Gaining dominance requires intentional angle shifts or off-balancing to disrupt the neutral setup.
3. Mutual Submission Access
Both practitioners can attack with leg locks such as heel hooks and ankle locks. This dual threat makes 50/50 a high-risk, high-reward position.
4. Positional Adjustments are Critical
Control comes from adjusting the hips, grips, or upper body posture to break symmetry. These adjustments can create dominance or open transitions to more advantageous positions.
5. Defensive Awareness
Since both players are vulnerable to submissions, hiding your heel and maintaining frame control is essential to avoid being countered.
6. Limited Mobility
The symmetrical nature of the entanglement can restrict movement, making it harder to create scrambles or transitions unless one player decisively shifts the balance.
7. Sweep and Transition Utility
50/50 is also used to off-balance opponents, initiate sweeps, or create entries into positions like Saddle or standing leg attacks when opponents shift weight or posture.

Cross Ashi Garami Position

The cross ashi garami is a leg entanglement position where the attacker traps the opponent’s far leg by threading their own leg across the opponent’s body, typically placing their foot on or near the opponent’s far hip while keeping their other leg underneath. This configuration creates strong control over one of the opponent’s legs while allowing the attacker to face the opponent more directly, often from a seated or supine position. Cross Ashi provides excellent access to inside heel hooks, straight ankle locks, and transitions to other entanglements like Inside Sankaku or Outside Ashi. It is particularly effective when attacking from guard or during scrambles, and is valued for its balance between control and dynamic entry options.

Cross Ashi Garami Principles

1. Far Leg Isolation

The entanglement targets your opponent’s far leg by threading your top leg across their body to trap and control it, limiting their mobility.

2. Foot Position Across the Hip

Your top foot should be planted across or near their far hip to create tension and restrict rotation, reinforcing control for submissions.

3. Strong Knee Line Control

Maintain the opponent’s knee inside your entanglement—this is vital to prevent escapes and to keep your attacking options alive.

4. Proper Angle (Perpendicular or Slightly Off-Center)

Aim to position your body slightly off to the side of the opponent’s leg to maximize torque for heel hooks and reduce defensive options.

5. Active Leg Engagement

Clamp your legs actively around the opponent’s leg—this engagement is key to retaining the position under pressure or while attacking.

6. Posture and Distance Management

Keep your hips close to your opponent's and manage the space wisely. Overextending weakens control and can allow escapes.

7. Transition Readiness

Be ready to transition to Inside Sankaku, 50/50, or Outside Ashi if your opponent defends. Fluid transitions are key to control and attack chains.


Ashi Garami Position

Ashi Garami, which translates as "leg entanglement" in Japanese, is the foundational leg control position in Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu and serves as the entry point to more advanced leg lock systems. In its standard form—often referred to as Single-Leg X—the attacker wraps one leg around the opponent’s leg while placing the foot of the other leg on the opponent’s far hip or thigh for control and elevation. This position offers a stable base to off-balance the opponent and initiate attacks such as straight ankle locks, or transitions to stronger positions like Outside Ashi, Inside Ashi, or Saddle. While Ashi Garami provides a strong platform for leg attacks, it is generally considered a transitional or setup position due to its more open structure and vulnerability to defensive counters if control is not maintained.

Ashi Garami Principles

Ashi Garami, meaning "leg entanglement" in Japanese, is a fundamental BJJ position that sets the stage for leg attacks and transitions. These principles explain how to control, attack, and advance from this foundational setup.

1. Leg Isolation
Ashi Garami begins by wrapping your leg around one of the opponent’s legs to isolate it. Your other foot usually posts on their far hip or thigh to control posture and spacing.
2. Elevation and Off-Balancing
The position works best when used to elevate the opponent’s leg and disrupt their base. This off-balancing effect creates openings for sweeps and transitions into stronger leg entanglements.
3. Open-Circuit Structure
Standard Ashi Garami is typically open-circuit, meaning the attacker’s legs are not fully locked around the opponent’s leg. This offers more movement but also less inherent security.
4. Control with Frames and Hooks
Maintaining frames with your hands and hooks with your feet allows you to manage distance and posture. Good frame control prevents guard passes and helps with submission setups.
5. Heel and Knee Line Awareness
Effective submissions depend on keeping your opponent’s knee line trapped within your entanglement and exposing their heel. Losing the knee line allows them to escape or nullify your attacks.
6. Defensive Vulnerabilities
Due to its open nature, Ashi Garami is vulnerable to guard passes, backsteps, and leg pummels if the attacker fails to maintain tight control or frame effectively.
7. Transitional Utility
Ashi Garami is often used as a starting point to progress into more dominant leg entanglements such as Outside Ashi, Inside Ashi, or Saddle. It provides both control and adaptability.

Double Trouble Control Principle

“Double Trouble” is not a leg entanglement position in itself, but a powerful control principle used to enhance the effectiveness of submissions. It plays a critical role in the modern leg lock game, particularly in systems influenced by the Danaher Death Squad and related instructional methodologies.

What is Double Trouble?
Double Trouble refers to the concept of simultaneously controlling both of your opponent’s legs—typically isolating the leg you are attacking and trapping the other to prevent them from rolling, bridging, or spinning out. It is commonly applied from dominant entanglements like Inside Sankaku or Saddle.
Is it a Position or a Concept?
Double Trouble is a strategic concept, not a standalone leg entanglement position. It is implemented within positions such as Saddle or Inside Ashi to secure the opponent and eliminate escape options, especially in submission-only formats.
Why is it Effective?
By trapping the second leg, Double Trouble prevents defensive counters like rolls, knee escapes, or back steps. This gives the attacker time and positional control to finish submissions—particularly heel hooks—without defensive interference.
Where Does It Fit in the Positional Hierarchy?
While not ranked like Inside Ashi or 50/50, Double Trouble enhances positional dominance. It is most commonly achieved from elite positions like Saddle, making it a force multiplier rather than a standalone position in the leg entanglement hierarchy.
